About the Indicator

What is SDG Indicator 17.8.1?

SDG Indicator 17.8.1 measures the proportion of individuals using the Internet. It is one of the official global indicators used to monitor progress toward Sustainable Development Goal 17: Partnerships for the Goals. Internet connectivity underpins education, employment, healthcare, finance and participation in the digital economy. Measuring who is connected — and who remains offline — helps identify where investment is still needed.

Impact

Why Internet Access Matters

📚
Education
Offline communities lack access to digital learning platforms, remote schooling, and the educational resources that increasingly define economic opportunity.
🏥
Healthcare
Telemedicine, health information, and digital disease surveillance all depend on connectivity — gaps in access translate directly into gaps in health outcomes.
💼
Employment
Digital connectivity is now a prerequisite for accessing remote work, online job markets, and the skills training needed for 21st-century labour markets.
💳
Digital Finance
Mobile banking, digital payments, and access to credit require internet access — without it, billions remain excluded from the formal financial system.
🏛️
Government Services
E-government platforms, social benefit portals, and civic participation increasingly rely on digital access — the offline are also often underserved by the state.
Summary

What the Data Tells Us

✓ Accelerating connectivity in the least connected regions will be essential if digital transformation is to support the Sustainable Development Goals and ensure that no one is left behind.
✓ Every region improved. No region declined; all SDG regional groupings recorded measurable gains in internet use between 2015 and 2024.
✓ Large disparities remain. The gap between the most connected region (Australia and New Zealand, 96%) and the least connected (Oceania excl. ANZ, 26%) stands at approximately 70 percentage points.
✓ LDCs continue to lag. Least Developed Countries reached only 31% in 2024 — 40 percentage points below the world average — with insufficient growth to close the gap by 2030.
✓ Without faster progress, billions remain excluded from digital opportunities in education, employment, healthcare, finance, and democratic participation.
